Occipital’s web and interaction design for Bridge headset apps

Occipital's Bridge Headset powered by Structure Sensor has an increasing number of available apps. Their product website needed an evergreen design that could transform into more of an apps store. After researching current solutions and customer needs, I created this UI design.

Case Study Summary

Problem Statement

There a multiple competitors in the market for virtual reality headsets. People are fascinated by the technology, and they typically enjoyed trying out a demo of Occipital's headset at events. Our marketing needed to go further to convince interested customers to purchase the headset because it had practical applications for their own projects. Showing the apps that customers could use with the Bridge headset was a way to demonstrate this usefulness.

My Role

My roles in creating the apps collection page were designer and developer. I created the wireframes, low fidelity mockups, high fidelity mockups, and built the page in HTML, LESS, and JQuery. My manager, a UX Engineer, provided guidance through the process as this was done during an UX design and front-end development internship. Business requirements came from the developer advocate. Both my manager and the developer advocate were in San Francisco while I worked in Boulder, CO.

Outcomes

Designing this page was one of the last pieces I worked on before finishing my internship at Occipital. The web page that launched was similar to but not exactly like the most recent design iteration I had completed before leaving. Moving forward, my next steps for evaluating the effectiveness of this page would be to monitor Google analytics to understand customer engagement with the content and if headset purchases increased within a set time frame after launching and promoting the page. I would also partner with the customer service team to monitor inquiries about the headset’s applications.
