Bridge Headset Apps Collection

Occipital's Bridge Headset powered by Structure Sensor has an increasing number of available apps. Their product website needed an evergreen design that could transform into more of an apps store. After researching current solutions and customer needs, I created this UI design.

Overview

Career context: While at the public library, my role was to be a technology guide for the public. I educated people on current and emerging technologies and the greater context of what they meant for us as a society and our future. However, I decided I wanted a more direct impact in the technology world as a developer and accepted a front-end development and UX design internship with Occipital. At this point, I was more aware of what UX design was but didnâ€™t understand the full practice or profession. One of my goals with my internship was to learn more about UX design from the Francisco UX designer at the company. In the end, my UX engineer manager was my primary mentor.

Occipital is a computer vision product company. One of their primary offerings is a mixed reality headset that their developer community can build mobile apps for. The developer advocate wanted a place on the Bridge headset website to feature these apps that would demonstrate the versatile uses of the headset.

Problem Statement

There a multiple competitors in the market for virtual reality headsets. People are fascinated by the technology and enjoyed trying out a demo of the headset at events. Our marketing needed to go further though to convince interested customers to purchase the headset because it was useful. Showing the apps that customers could use with the app was a way to demonstrate this usefulness.

Users and Audience

For designing the layout of the apps collection, I wanted to focus on making certain the Bridge headset's various application areas were clear. This would help potential customers see that the headset had a variety of software available as well as offering an innovative experience.

Another consideration the layout needed to account for was scalability and being future-proof enough so that update requests from the marketing department would be quick and easy to implement for the web engineering team.

Methods: Interface Design

For the interface design, I considered the connection between the customer's experience on Occipital's website and their experience in the iOS or Android app stores linked to the website. They would need a visual cue upon navigating away that would connect the two experiences. To solve this problem I experimented with the proper placement of the app icon and selecting a informative screenshot for each app. The app screenshot also showed the user what their experience of the app might be like and provided evidence that the Bridge headset worked with relevant, useful software.

Outcomes

The final design launched on the website was a card style layout more in keeping with one of the initial iterations of the design. The app screenshot is the focus of each app listing with an icon and brief description below. A slight animation on each card shows the user the item is clickable so they can navigate to its detail page.
